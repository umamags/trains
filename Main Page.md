This is exactly the kind of project where a collection of facts becomes much more interesting when organized around a single question.

The question I'd build the documentary around is:

> **"How does one of the world's largest railway systems move 25 million people every day?"**

Everything else becomes part of answering that question.

# Think like a systems architect, not a historian

Knowing your interests, I wouldn't make this a historical documentary.

I'd make it a **systems documentary.**

Think of it as peeling layers of an onion.

Layer 1

> What passengers see

Layer 2

> What railway employees do

Layer 3

> How stations work

Layer 4

> How trains are operated

Layer 5

> How everything is coordinated

Layer 6

> How the government funds it

Layer 7

> How the whole ecosystem evolved

That's much more engaging.

Don't think of this as making **one 10-minute documentary**.

Think of it as creating **your own miniature "Indian Railways University."**

Imagine building a repository with:

- 300 interconnected notes
    
- 100 diagrams
    
- 50 timelines
    
- 25 maps
    
- 100 photographs
    
- 40 short animations
    
- 30 interviews
    
- A glossary of railway terminology
    

The 10-minute documentary then becomes just one expression of that deeper knowledge. You could later create focused episodes on signaling, locomotive manufacturing, station operations, freight logistics, famous routes, or the economics of the railway system.

Given your fascination with complex systems and your upcoming retirement, this could become a long-term intellectual project: documenting Indian Railways the way a systems architect would—showing not just _what_ it is, but _how_ thousands of interconnected parts work together to keep millions of people and vast quantities of freight moving every day.
